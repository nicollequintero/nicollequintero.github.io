Ignorance was Bliss

August 10, 2014

I grew up in a city whose diversity I couldn't fully appreciate until later in life. As a kid, my best friends were Vietnamese, black, portuguese, white, Puerto Rican and Ecuadorian. I, someone who identifies as Latina, was a member of and eventually president, of the Asian club in high school. All this is to say that I didn't consider that my ethnicity as a defining part of who I was, at least not at the top of the list of all the other things I considered myself: a nerd, a techie, an avid reader, etc. If you would've asked me back then, I would've said my ethnicity played little part in how I maneuvered through life. Though idealistic, I certainly didn't have a realistic view of race and ethnicity or of what being an ethnic minority in America meant. Then I went to collegeâ€¦ and boy was I in for a rude awakening.

I took psychology classes from the start and ended up majoring in it, with a concentration in social psychology. For two years I worked as the assistant manager of a research laboratory that focused on threatened identities. Stereotype threat is a subject I studied ad nauseam. But more than that, it really fucked with my head.

The thing is, before I learned about stereotype threat, implicit biases and the myriad cognitive biases that govern our social world, I lived a fairly ignorant and carefree life. Of course I realized that I was the only girl in my computer science classes and that my male classmates wanted to dethrone me as the top student. I even realized part of that was simply because I was a girl. And I also noticed the exchange program we had with students in a wealthy, mostly white suburb of Connecticut was a social experiment in bringing the "disadvantaged" and "advantaged" youths together (cue the "West Side Story" overture). But it wasn't until really studying these issues in college that I realized my experience wasn't limited to a few overly competitive boys in high school. It would follow me. Everywhere.

I learned that in all of my math and science classes I'd just be expected to perform less well than boys. I learned that in my reading and writing classes I'd just be expected to be less well read than my white classmates. I learned that I couldn't administer certain tests for my own research laboratory because as a Latina I'd be perceived as an immigrant and bias the state of mind of the test-takers. I learned that at my part-time accounting job I'd be immediately judged as reliable and immature just for being young.

Damn, ignorance was bliss.

Maybe it was the perfect storm of knowledge, youthful angst and I'm-in-college-so-I-have-to-find-myself-now, but the weight of all that judgment weighed me down. Don't get me wrong, I played out my dutiful Type A role and did just fine in my classes, met a lot of people and excelled in my major. But I definitely held back at times. There's the scholarships I didn't apply for thinking I'd never qualify. There are the discussion groups where I stood silent lest I speak for my entire person-kind and be judged, or worse, cause judgement on said people. There were the extra few heart palpitations before an exam or group presentation.

I won't even offer an attempt to solve these issues. We all have our hangups and these are (just some of) mine. But one thing I know for sure is that THIS helps. Talking and listening. I only wish I could've had the freedom and safe space to talk about the things that (even mildly) plagued me back then. I never in my wildest dreams expected we'd be talking about stereotype threat at DBC and it makes me love it so much more for that. If I had to guess, I'd bet the catharsis of taking the anxious energy, balling it up and putting it back out into the ether would've helped back then. I hope we can all learn to respect and listen to our peers and give them that opportunity if nothing else.
